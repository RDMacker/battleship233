# Content Think Tank: gsmbc, 2026-07-14

Mountain bike coaching content right now is being pulled by two forces at once, a live World Cup season heavy on psychology and a search landscape rewriting what earns a click. On the racing side the sharpest story is Jordan Williams, who went one thousand, one hundred and seven days without a Downhill World Cup win and then took two inside eight days, at La Thuile in early July and Pal Arinsal on the 11th and 12th, after coming back from a broken collarbone. Valentina Höll won five of six rounds, the first elite woman to manage that since Rachel Atherton in 2016, while reigning world champion Jackson Goldstone did not reach a podium until round seven, which turns the whole season into a running argument about process consistency versus raw speed. Underneath the racing, coaching is arguing with itself about two things. The old "lean back on the steep stuff" cue is being taken apart everywhere at once, by Zep's Mythbusters and by Shoulders of Giants calling it the deadliest sin, on the mechanism that leaning back straightens the arms and strips out the range a rider needs to absorb a drop. And a certification-versus-teaching debate has gone loud, with the NSMB forum calling the PMBI badge a solid framework that still has no mentorship or teach-the-teacher layer, and Lee McCormack's March 2026 Substack pushing hard on untrained, unpaid volunteer youth coaches. On distribution the ground has moved. The people who study answer engines, the CXL team among them, find that pages opening each section with a plain direct answer earn roughly 2.8 times the AI citations, that most citations go to pages refreshed within the past year, and that Google's AI Overviews are cutting organic clicks by fifteen to forty-six percent, hitting "how to" and "why" searches hardest. On Instagram the July 2026 numbers are blunt, carousels are being saved around nine times more often than Reels, which argues for keeping the saveable, returnable format and making it actually teach something.

## The slate

### Blog

**1. Should You Lean Back on Steep Descents? Why the Answer Flipped When Droppers Took Over**
*Answer-first blog pillar. Boldness 1/5.*
**Concept:** Every section opens with a plain one or two sentence answer a chatbot can lift, then the coaching reasoning expands underneath, with a visible refresh date on a calendar. The piece leads with the one part that is actually fresh, the mechanism, that leaning back made sense on a high-saddle bike but on a dropper post it straightens the arms and strips out the range a rider needs to absorb a drop. It targets a high-volume misconception people type into search rather than restating a busted cue.
**Hook:** Watch a rider hit a steep roll-in with locked, straight arms and you can see the exact moment the bike, not the rider, decides what happens next.
**Why now:** Structured answer-first pages earn roughly 2.8 times the AI citations and most go to pages refreshed within a year, while AI Overviews cut clicks hardest on "how to" and "why" searches, and the lean-back cue is being dismantled across coaching right now.
**Critics flagged:** the "don't lean back" message is everywhere, so the dropper-history mechanism has to lead or the piece reads as one more mythbust.
**Merge:** part of the answer-first pair with idea 5, which is the program that houses individual pieces like this.

**2. What "Light Hands, Heavy Feet" Is Actually Asking Your Nervous System To Do**
*Answer-first blog post, with an optional annotated carousel cut-down. Boldness 3/5.*
**Concept:** Takes the three cues riders love to mock (light hands heavy feet, look where you want to go, stay loose) and treats the mockery as the real question, giving each its own section that opens with a plain answer to what the phrase instructs the body to do, then why the shorthand fails anyone never shown the underlying action. It neither defends nor trashes the clichés, it translates them, and it goes materially deeper than any short clip so it earns its place as the searchable version. The frame-one, send-this-to-your-buddy mechanic from the video pitches survives here as an annotated carousel cut-down rather than a talk-to-camera Reel, which GSMBC's Instagram does not run.
**Hook:** Every rider who has taken a lesson can recite "light hands, heavy feet," and almost none of them can tell you what it asks their body to do, which is exactly why they are still white-knuckling the bars on the next root.
**Why now:** the mtbr "most common coaching tips" thread has riders trading and half-mocking these exact phrases, so the audience is already saying the cues feel hollow.
**Critics flagged:** the topic is web-saturated and overlaps two video pitches, so it only earns a slot by going deeper than any reel could.
**Merge:** merges the cue-decode cluster (analyst-6, producer-5, insider-1) into one franchise, with the blog as the on-channel home and a carousel as the visual cut-down, not Reels.

**3. Your Body Is Ready For The Drop. Your Nervous System Filed A Complaint.**
*Blog post. Boldness 2/5.*
**Concept:** Separates two things riders and coaches blend, the technical skill to ride a feature and the fear left over from the last time it went wrong, and argues that treating post-crash fear as a technique gap (more reps, more body-position cues) often makes it worse because the body already owns the movement. It describes what rebuilds the link between a capable body and a mind that has filed the feature under danger, and it stays in coaching voice so it never wanders into the mental-performance lane that belongs to sportsense.
**Hook:** You rode it clean a hundred times, then you crashed on it once, and now your body still knows exactly how to do it while your brain refuses to let you, and that is not you losing skill.
**Why now:** the Squamish practice The Mind Mountain, which pairs a PMBIA coach with a counselling psychologist, described a client haunted by a past accident whose body was fit but mind was not on board, and sports psychology has gone mainstream enough for riders to hear fear as separate and trainable.
**Critics flagged:** a resonant idea that got pitched twice, so it needs the sharper "nervous system filed a complaint" framing to stand on its own.
**Merge:** merges the two fear-after-crash blogs (insider-5, thought-leader-3) into one.

**4. It Took Jordan Williams 1,107 Days to Win Again. What Actually Rebuilt the Confidence?**
*Sports-psychology blog post, answer-first. Boldness 2/5.*
**Concept:** Leads with the evergreen question riders actually search, how to get your nerve back, and uses Williams as the way in rather than the whole spine, because the downhill-pro-to-amateur leap is thin on its own. It uses his real sequence, 1,107 days without a win, a broken collarbone, then two wins in eight days, to separate the parts of confidence a coach can influence (structured exposure, process goals) from vague "believe in yourself" advice, and it keeps the mental angle in coaching territory, clear of sportsense. Ship within days while the story is hot, or strip the peg and run the mechanism evergreen.
**Hook:** On July 11, Jordan Williams won a Downhill World Cup run for the second time in eight days, and before that he had gone one thousand, one hundred and seven days without winning one at all.
**Why now:** the back-to-back wins landed this week, La Thuile in early July and Pal Arinsal on the 11th and 12th, so search interest is peaking and the answer-first structure rides the AEO shift.
**Critics flagged:** it leans on a news peg that is already closing and a pro-to-amateur leap, so lead evergreen and treat Williams as the hook.

**5. Answer on the First Line**
*Standing blog column and library. Boldness 2/5.*
**Concept:** A standing column where every entry answers one narrow question a rider would type into a chatbot before a race or lesson, in the first two sentences, before the prose expands into reasoning and a named client case no AI summary can compress, which is the real moat. Each entry carries a visible last-reviewed date on a loose quarterly refresh so maintenance stays realistic, and the answers stay in flowing prose so the column never becomes a bulleted FAQ. Over time the entries index into a library organised by skill and situation, the reference that coaches chasing social reach never build.
**Hook:** A sample entry reads "Should you brake before the corner or in it? Before it, almost always, and here is the one exception that makes good riders think otherwise," then the prose expands.
**Why now:** the CXL team found structured answer-first pages earn roughly 2.8 times the citations with most going to pages refreshed within a year, while jesscreatives notes most coaches leave niche question-answering wide open while chasing Instagram.
**Critics flagged:** the answer-first library is the most-recommended play of the year and risks reading as textbook, so the named client case that no AI can compress has to be the differentiator.
**Merge:** the answer-first pair, this is the program, idea 1 is the flagship entry that lives inside it.

### Pinkbike

**6. The Riders With the Most to Gain Ride the Oldest Gear**
*Pinkbike single-claim opinion feature. Boldness 4/5.*
**Concept:** The revealed-choice argument that the riders with the most to gain from a marginal edge are the most conservative adopters, because at their level skill dwarfs equipment. At a Eurobike that drew about half its usual exhibitors, with new eCVT drives and 32-inch prototypes free on the floor, not one pro XC racer picked them up, and Reece Wilson took second against factory riders on a belt-drive gearbox bike, while the gear-chasing amateur is the one DirtSmart says spends the most time in the emergency room. Verify the Eurobike and Reece Wilson facts before pitching, because that audience fact-checks hard and the whole piece rests on them.
**Hook:** Every new drive and a set of 32-inch wheels sat on the Eurobike floor this summer, free for any pro who wanted an edge, and not one of the fast ones picked them up.
**Why now:** Eurobike 2026 came in at roughly half its usual exhibitors, no pro XC racer chose the 32-inch prototypes, and Reece Wilson finished 2.3 seconds short of the unclaimed Belted Purse in second on a gearbox bike.
**Critics flagged:** "skill beats gear" is an old thesis, so the piece lives or dies on the revealed-choice evidence being airtight and checked.

**7. Your Coach Passed the Course. That Was Never the Hard Part.**
*Pinkbike single-claim opinion feature. Boldness 5/5.*
**Concept:** The certification is a safety baseline and a framework, not a teaching apprenticeship, and the coaches who know it best say the pathway has no mentorship and no teach-the-teacher layer, which leaves a field of people who ride well and assume that settles it. Riding ability is necessary and nowhere near sufficient, and the two are separate trainable skills. It is credible precisely because Rich holds the certifications he is criticising and can name what the syllabus does and does not build.
**Hook:** I hold the certifications, and I can also tell you that the day I passed them I was not yet good at the actual job, which is not riding a bike but changing how someone else rides one.
**Why now:** the NSMB thread calling the PMBI cert a solid framework that lacks mentorship, alongside Lee McCormack's March 2026 Substack on untrained, unpaid volunteer youth coaches, has moved the conversation from which drill to who is qualified to teach.
**Critics flagged:** three overlapping cert pitches would have cannibalised each other, so this self-implicating version is the single keeper and Pinkbike will run only one per cycle.
**Merge:** merges the cert-versus-teaching cluster (insider-3, thought-leader-2) into one; the react-to-own-footage take (maverick-6) is distinct and held in Wildcards.

**8. Brake Lever Reach Is the Most Overlooked Control on Your Bike, and Three Millimetres Changes Everything**
*Pinkbike single-claim opinion feature. Boldness 2/5.*
**Concept:** A narrow single-claim feature built on one measurement and one rider case from Rich's coaching, that riders obsess over suspension and tyre pressure while leaving brake levers wherever the shop set them, and that pulling reach in a few millimetres changes finger position, grip security and arm pump. It rests on a single student whose "grip strength problem" turned out to be a lever-reach problem, one claim, one number, personal data, not a listicle. Pitch this one strong piece first rather than a standing column, and keep the 3mm anecdote to this feature alone so it does not get burned across the newsletter and the carousel.
**Hook:** A rider came to me convinced she needed to train grip strength for steep descents, when her forearms were pumping up because her brake levers sat a few millimetres too far from the bar.
**Why now:** Pinkbike's current top template is exactly this narrow single-claim personal-data piece, with Jessie-May Morgan's handlebar-width feature as live proof, and the March 2026 core update rewards one sharp mechanism over broad advice.
**Critics flagged:** the "3mm brake lever" claim appeared four times across the slate and copies the Morgan template closely, so it only ships as one disciplined feature that owns the measurement.
**Merge:** merges the brake-lever repetition (analyst-2, producer-3, and the echoes in the newsletter and carousel) into one Pinkbike feature; the other channels use a different number.

### Instagram

**9. The Course Schedule That Doubles as a Micro-Lesson**
*Monthly Canva carousel. Boldness 4/5.*
**Concept:** The monthly course-schedule carousel rebuilt as a technique tasting menu, where each month is built around one tiny named mechanism delivered as a real micro-lesson, the dates become the slide-two payoff, and the cover is a five-to-eight-word curiosity line. Two covers get designed up front, a primary and an alternate, so the guaranteed re-serve on a fresh cover becomes a real A/B test instead of a wasted second chance. The template stays fixed (cover promise, slide-two dates, slide-three onward on the one skill), so the only recurring work is the swappable line, and the women's skills clinic is one instance of this same template if a skills-first clinic is on the calendar.
**Hook:** Cover, no dates visible, one tight photo of a foot on a pedal and the line "Your outside foot is loading a beat too late." Slide two delivers the mechanism and the August dates, slide three is the schedule.
**Why now:** July 2026 data shows carousels saved around nine times more than Reels, the platform hands original work 40 to 60 percent more reach while penalising reposts, and winning covers run a short curiosity gap that pays off on slide two.
**Critics flagged:** a curiosity cover over an unchanged schedule earns no saves, so the month has to deliver an actual micro-lesson, and the monthly writing load is the one real cost.
**Merge:** merges the four carousel-schedule ideas (analyst-3, insider-4, producer-1, maverick-5) into one; the micro-lesson leads, the design-both-covers discipline bolts on, and the women's-clinic version is one edition. It uses a non-brake-lever mechanism so it does not repeat the Pinkbike feature.

**10. The Film Room, One Corner at a Time**
*Recurring Canva carousel, one moment per edition. Boldness 3/5.*
**Concept:** A recurring carousel that takes a single corner or braking moment and walks through it one annotated frame per slide, arrows and body-position marks on each still, so the swipe becomes the slow motion and the edition closes on the one thing a weekend rider can copy. It uses Rich's own coaching footage or properly licensed images rather than lifted World Cup broadcast stills, which is a rights problem and not a free asset, and it stays on one moment per edition so the per-weekend annotation load stays sustainable. Because carousels are saved far more than short video, it is built to be kept and returned to.
**Hook:** Slide one is a single freeze-frame at the entry to a rutted corner, one arrow, and the line "She has already done the important thing here, and it happened before the corner," with a prompt to swipe.
**Why now:** July Instagram data shows carousels saved around nine times more than Reels, the anti-repost shift rewards original annotated work, and BBALLBREAKDOWN has built fourteen years of trust on freeze-frame arrow-annotated possessions without anything like it crossing into MTB coaching.
**Critics flagged:** lifting broadcast stills is a rights trap, so this only works on owned or licensed footage, and the annotation load per weekend has to stay light.

### Email

**11. The Rider Who Spends The Most Time In The Emergency Room**
*Single-voice Thursday newsletter. Boldness 4/5.*
**Concept:** A newsletter to the fit, strong rider who has hit a plateau and quietly noticed their crash count creeping up, stating plainly that a plateau arriving alongside more frequent crashes is almost never fitness and almost always technique. It walks through why the fittest riders can carry a bad habit fast enough to get hurt, and why they are the least likely to book a lesson because they read speed as proof, then closes on one self-check, which technical section am I riding fastest right before I crash on it.
**Hook:** If your fitness is climbing and your crash count is climbing with it, I have bad news about which one is causing the other.
**Why now:** DirtSmart wrote about exactly this rider, the self-described expert who buys every gimmick but never instruction and spends the most time in the emergency room once a fitness plateau meets a bad habit.
**Critics flagged:** a touch self-serving and it shares a source with the gear-skepticism piece, but it is the strongest email on the slate and ships as pure prose with zero production.

**12. The Ride Where Everything Clicked Is Not A Plan**
*Single-voice newsletter. Boldness 3/5.*
**Concept:** Reframes two things sold as performance tools, chasing flow every ride and leaning on a pre-race ritual or positive mantra, with the plain claim that flow is an outcome no one can reliably summon and rituals mostly build dependence, so a rider whose good days rest on either is building on sand. It swaps the myth for what travels ride to ride, and every point re-anchors in a physical on-bike cue and Rich's own client cases so it stays in coaching voice and never drifts into the pre-move-routine language that would read as sportsense marketing.
**Hook:** You are chasing the ride where everything clicked, and even sports psychologists cannot reliably produce that feeling on command, which means you have built your riding on the one thing you cannot control.
**Why now:** Flow Mountain Bike ran a "Sports Psychology Myths in Popular Use" piece arguing that chasing flow is chasing what psychologists cannot reliably produce and that mantras and rituals build dependence, and riders are passing it around as technique.
**Critics flagged:** hanging a whole piece on one article is aggregation, so Rich's own client data has to carry the information gain, and the framing has to stay on-bike to keep clear of the sportsense lane.
**Merge:** merges the flow/ritual-myth pair (insider-6, thought-leader-4) into one email; the blog twin drifted toward coaching-software territory, which is why this stays anchored in physical cues.

**13. One Small Thing, Every Thursday**
*Fixed Thursday newsletter segment. Boldness 2/5.*
**Concept:** A fixed named segment where the whole edition is built around one small adjustment and nothing else, in Rich's single voice with no roundup or padding, small enough to try next ride and explained deeply enough the reader feels why it works. The fixed shape (what most riders do, the one change, how to feel it next ride) makes it fast to write and instantly recognisable, and the flagship edition uses a non-brake-lever adjustment so it does not repeat the Pinkbike feature.
**Hook:** The opening line reads "This whole email is about where your eyes are in the back half of the turn, and it is the difference between the bike following your line and the bike choosing it."
**Why now:** beehiiv found single-voice letters convert between 5 and 25 percent with the direction moving toward relationship depth, and comedy's shift to hyper-specific material gives permission to build a whole edition around one small change.
**Critics flagged:** the original lead example was (again) 3mm brake-lever reach, so it needed a different flagship adjustment, which it now has.

**14. What Ryan Pinkerton Does in the Start Gate Before a World Cup Run**
*Single-voice Thursday newsletter. Boldness 2/5.*
**Concept:** Rich pulls the specific named mental-performance techniques Ryan Pinkerton described about start-gate pressure, and the spine is Rich choosing which two or three transfer to what an amateur can rehearse before a race run or hard trailhead, and, just as important, naming which ones do not carry to club-rider scale and why. It is not a podcast recap, it is Rich's selection in his own voice, kept in coaching territory and separate from sportsense. Ship it fast, because the podcast peg is already a couple of weeks old, or run the transfer idea evergreen.
**Hook:** Ryan Pinkerton is still chasing his first elite World Cup win, and a couple of weeks ago he described exactly what he does in the ten seconds before the gate drops, and most of it works on a Tuesday trail night too.
**Why now:** the Downtime Podcast interview landed on June 29 with Pinkerton on start-gate pressure, and 2026 newsletters reward single-voice relationship depth.
**Critics flagged:** "elite routine translated for amateurs" is one of the most-made newsletter formats and the peg decays fast, so the non-transferable picks are what make it Rich's rather than generic.

## Wildcards worth the risk

**Your Fastest Corner and Your Slowest Corner Are the Same Corner**
*Pinkbike single-claim opinion feature. Boldness 5/5.* Poker's mental-game coaches put a dollar figure on tilt by type, and Jared Tendler argues that shifting 20 percent of sessions from C-game to A-game roughly doubles win rate, which reframes consistency as a measurable gap. This feature takes one rider and one named section of trail, times their best clean run against their worst still-rideable run on that same feature across a session, and reads the difference the way a poker coach reads a leak, arguing the fix lives in the bad runs nobody films. The risk runs two ways: capturing timed best and worst runs plus body-position reads is heavy for a solo coach, and the timing-and-measurement angle can start to look like a sportsense software demo, so it has to run on one student and one clean session, framed explicitly as coaching with poker as a light touch and real seconds from one rider up front. It is worth taking because the A-to-C-game leak lens has never crossed into MTB coaching and "your slowest run on this corner costs you something specific" is a far sharper idea than any generic consistency talk. Hook: a poker coach can tell you, to the dollar, what your worst hour at the table cost you, and ask most riders what their worst corner costs them and you get a shrug.

**Rich Against Rich: Reading My Own Old Footage**
*Pinkbike feature or Instagram annotated-stills carousel. Boldness 5/5.* The strongest musician content now is artists reacting to their own early work and sitting with instincts they have outgrown, and this imports that posture into coaching, a field that never admits an outgrown instinct. Rich takes old footage of his own riding and early coaching, freeze-frames it in an arrow-annotated style, and argues with his younger self about specific mechanics, where past-Rich cued something now known to be wrong and what changed his mind, which doubles as a case study in ride-ability versus teach-ability. The risk is that it only works if usable old footage actually exists, and it sits near the crowded cert cluster, so if the footage is there it ships as annotated stills and if it is not it is undeliverable. It is worth taking because a coach publicly admitting an outgrown instinct is rare, react-to-your-own-work is a proven format elsewhere, and it is a more original vehicle for the ride-versus-teach point than the straight cert essay. Hook: a freeze-frame of Rich mid-descent from years ago, a red arrow on his straightened arms, and the caption "This is me, this is wrong, let me show you what I was missing."

**The Ride You're Not Doing This Year**
*Single-voice newsletter series. Boldness 5/5.* Loud budgeting inverts goal-setting, so instead of listing what you want to add you state publicly and specifically what you refuse to spend money and attention on, and the refusal itself is the accountability. This newsletter does the same with riding, where Rich states his own limit first (what he is not working on this year, the new bike, the A-line, the skills he is deliberately ignoring), then optionally features readers' commitments the following week so the refusal is witnessed. The risk is that it depends on readers publicly replying and being featured and it flops if few respond, so Rich's own stated list has to carry the edition on its own and reader features are upside, sequenced once the list is warm. It is worth taking because inverting loud budgeting into naming what you refuse to work on is fresh for a coaching letter, it maps onto DirtSmart's gear-chasing rider who buys everything except instruction, and public specific commitment drives replies better than another tip. Hook: here is what I am not working on this year, said out loud so you can hold me to it.

**The Five Doors Bad Riding Advice Walks Through**
*Occasional standalone blog posts, not a committed series. Boldness 5/5.* Climate and health communicators inoculate against misinformation using FLICC (fake experts, logical fallacies, impossible expectations, cherry picking, conspiracy), and this imports the framework into riding technique, where each post takes one piece of bad advice, names which of the five doors it walked through, then supplies what the evidence and a coaching eye actually say. The risk is that the taxonomy can add overhead a reader does not want (one critic would kill it as a brand conceit), and committing to a second standing series would overload a solo operator on top of the answer-first library, so it runs as occasional standalone posts with the framework kept light enough that it never reads like homework. It is worth taking because pre-bunking one bad claim with a named, repeatable structure is real white space, and the lean-back cue is a ready-made first target that the mythbust wave has already primed. Hook: under a plain header, "'Lean back on the steep stuff' is bad advice, and it is bad in a specific, nameable way, and here is which one."

## Killed, and why

The cull was mostly about sameness. The room produced the same good idea several times over, so four carousel-schedule redesigns collapsed into one, three cert-versus-teaching pitches into one, two fear-after-crash blogs into one, two flow-and-ritual pieces into one, and the brake-lever "3mm" claim showed up four separate times before it was pinned to a single Pinkbike feature. The two ideas hard-killed on votes, the plainest carousel redesign and the second fear-after-crash blog, died precisely because a stronger twin already carried the argument. The second pattern was off-channel asks, most visibly the lone YouTube pitch on a brand that runs no YouTube channel, and the talk-to-camera Reels concepts on an Instagram that publishes Canva carousels, both of which asked Rich to stand up a new production capability rather than make content. The third was thin or decaying news pegs, where the Pinkerton podcast and the Jordan Williams window both survive only if shipped within days or stripped back to their evergreen mechanism. The last one, and the one worth watching in future sessions, was ideas that quietly assume an offer or an audience that may not exist: the junior late-specialisation piece only pays off if parents of juniors are actually on the list or the angle is reframed to adult cross-training, and the women's-clinic polemic only lands if a skills-first women's clinic is really on the calendar rather than criticising other clubs with nothing to sell. A quieter trap sat underneath a few otherwise strong ideas, the drift toward selling coaching software, which is why the flow-myth email and the timed-runs feature both had to be re-anchored in physical on-bike cues and one-student coaching so GSMBC content stays strictly separate from sportsense.

## If you only make three things this month

1. The lean-back-on-descents blog pillar (idea 1). It rides the answer-first shift while the mythbust wave is live, it is pure writing on GSMBC's own channel, and it catches a question riders type into search every day.
2. The tasting-menu course-schedule carousel (idea 9). It upgrades the one thing GSMBC already publishes every month into something people save, at no new channel or production cost, and the two-cover design makes the guaranteed re-serve pay.
3. The emergency-room-rider newsletter (idea 11). It is the strongest email on the slate, it ships as prose with zero production, and it stops the fit, plateauing rider cold with a diagnosis they have quietly suspected.
