# Content Think Tank: sportsense — 17 July 2026

Bouldering is having its loudest, most watchable summer in years, and almost none of that attention has been turned into coaching content yet. The IFSC Youth World Championships are live in Arco, Italy from 18 to 25 July with boulder qualifications landing 22 to 23 July, and Climbing Escalade Canada has sent a full team backed by an unusually large five-coach staff, which is itself the story because five coaches at a Youth Worlds is still considered a lot in a sport this under-coached. The senior season already has its 2026 shape, with Annie Sanders the most consistent winner, Sorato Anraku opening on three straight golds, Janja Garnbret taking a historic 50th World Cup title at Innsbruck on 21 June, and Canada's Oscar Baudrand reaching his first ever World Cup boulder final in Madrid at the end of May after nearly quitting, a milestone he credits to moving to a Montreal gym whose training finally matched competition. Underneath the results, the structure is shifting fast, with bouldering confirmed as its own standalone Olympic medal at LA 2028 (qualifying from 2027), the governing body rebranded to World Climbing on an access-over-elite strategy, and the first formal performance-coaching certification only arriving in Fall 2026. The live audience conversation is skepticism toward the 2026 wave of AI apps that stamp a 0-to-100 score on a climb, and the accepted community truth that filming yourself only shows what you did, never what a stronger climber would have done on the same problem. That gap, between rising stakes and thin coaching, is exactly where sportsense speaks, because its native move, two clips overlaid on the same boulder in about thirty seconds, is a coach owning the read rather than an algorithm handing back a number.

## The slate

### Instagram

**guess the grade** — Reel (repeatable weekly template), boldness 3. A silent boulder clip runs and the viewer guesses the V-grade or the comp round before a hard cut to the reveal, where the coach freezes on the pose overlay and points to the two or three tells that gave it away, usually foot precision and hip position. It is engineered for the two things that drive reach in 2026, replay (the reveal sits at the end so people rewatch to check the tells) and DM shares (a gym climber sends it to their partner to argue the grade), and the caption still teaches a real tell so it earns the save even from someone who never guesses. The honest reveal of a human coach reading movement is the exact counter to the AI scorers.
- Hook: "guess the grade before the feet cut" (over the frozen crux, then the clip plays to the move).
- Why now: The 2026 flood of AI apps that score movement is exactly what climbers are told to distrust, and chess's guess-the-elo is still peaking (GothamChess posted a new version in April 2026), so a format that puts a human coach's read at the payoff rides the live argument.
- Critics: the strongest pure-engagement bet on the slate, the one concept built to make the feed play rather than watch, and genuinely climbing-native.

**the heel hook was brilliant, the feet were a blunder** — carousel (8 to 10 slides), boldness 5. One boulder attempt annotated move by move in the grading language chess players know from engine review, a green Brilliant on the heel hook that held the swing, an Inaccuracy on the early hip break, a Blunder on the cut feet, with each slide freezing one move of a Canadian youth climber overlaid on a World Cup finalist on the same problem. What separates it from every AI app in the feed is that the grades are the coach's read written over the overlay, not a number an algorithm invented, so the annotations must stay visibly human-written or the ad accidentally becomes the scoring gimmick it is attacking. Each swipe reveals the next graded move, which is the save-and-forward behaviour coaches and filming parents already do.
- Hook: "the heel hook was brilliant, the feet were a blunder, here is the same boulder graded move by move."
- Why now: Chess's Brilliant and Blunder annotations are among the most recognizable analysis language online, and borrowing it while keeping the marks visibly coach-written draws the exact line the community is arguing about between being scored and being coached.
- Critics: the sharpest concept on the board, a format transplant that is actually a twist and the cleanest wedge against the AI-scoring apps.

**an 87 out of 100 isn't coaching** — single image, boldness 3. A mock AI grade card reads "87 / 100" with a bright green line struck through it, set beside a real coach's voice note and a marked-up overlay frozen on the heel that released, the same flash attempt turned into two very different outputs. The caption explains the mechanism honestly, that a score compresses a whole attempt into one figure you cannot act on, while a coach can stop on the exact frame where the heel cut and give you the one change, and it keeps the named competitor apps out of the on-image copy to avoid picking fights in paid creative.
- Hook: "scored 87 out of 100, never once mentioned the heel that cut."
- Why now: The 2026 wave of pose-estimation scorers and the community's told-to-be-skeptical reflex make this the cleanest single-image statement of what separates sportsense from the graders.
- Critics: trivially buildable in Canva and the clearest visual on the slate for drawing the score-versus-coaching line.

**same boulder, two betas, one holds the swing** — Reel (30 seconds), boldness 2. This is the F1 ghost-car overlay imported to bouldering, a Canadian youth boulderer's attempt overlaid as a ghost on an Arco or World Cup finalist on the same style of coordination problem, narrating the single move where the swing leaks, the finalist dropping a knee to kill the barn-door while the club climber's hips stay square and cut the feet. It is cut to loop cleanly with the payoff before second fifteen and the whole claim riding as first-frame text over a frozen overlay, and because a Canadian youth climber and an Arco finalist are almost never on the identical boulder, the caption anchors to the same move-style rather than overstating a literal shared problem. This is the reference build that merges the near-identical analyst-1, producer-1 and thought-leader-3 overlay ads into one; the mirror-not-a-map line, that your phone shows what you did and not what to change, is folded into its caption.
- Hook: "same boulder, two betas, watch which one keeps the swing."
- Why now: Arco is live this week with boulder quals around 22 to 23 July, so the ad borrows real event attention and draws Kraig's exact world-stage-to-gym line, with Baudrand's first Canadian World Cup boulder final anchoring the training-against-what-you-are-chasing mechanism.
- Critics: the killer demo made real, the exact overlay Kraig responded to, deliverable by one person in the app.

**the difference between beta spray and being coached** — two-panel single image, boldness 4. The left panel is a busy commercial gym with the fire-extinguisher "do not spray me" gesture and the words "beta you didn't ask for," the right panel a phone showing a private, marked-up review from the athlete's own coach with a skeletal overlay on the hips and the words "an overlay you did." The caption draws one clean line, that a stranger handing you the solution robs you of the read that builds you, while an invited overlay shows you where your hips break early without taking the problem away, and it is written so the borrowed etiquette issue reads as respect for the climber rather than opportunism.
- Hook: "beta you didn't ask for, versus an overlay you did."
- Why now: The beta-spray etiquette fight is a live 2026 gym conversation, with climbers using a fire-extinguisher signal and the sprayed disproportionately being women, kids and those judged by body type.
- Critics: the most genuinely-of-the-culture idea on the board, a clean invited-versus-intrusive line almost nobody has built an ad on.

**your feet cut because your hips are square to the wall** — talking-head Reel, boldness 1. A coach reel that opens with one named mistake in bold text over a paused green pose overlay before the coach explains the single mechanism, that square hips hand the swing a long lever while turning one hip into the wall shortens it, and only at the end mentions that the whole review came from a clip the athlete submitted from another city. That closing beat is the real pitch, because it shows the relationship staying two-way and coach-controlled rather than a one-way broadcast.
- Hook: "your feet cut because your hips are square to the wall" (held two seconds before the coach speaks).
- Why now: The talking-head-plus-on-screen-text reel is the dominant 2026 educational format for coaches, and naming the mistake in text first is what earns the saves.
- Critics: the strongest coach-audience idea and the cheapest to shoot, delivering the two-way infrastructure pitch without feature-dumping.

**the v-grade is a lagging indicator** — before-and-after Reel, boldness 4. The same young climber on the same spray-wall problem from the same taped fixed angle a month apart, with a skeletal overlay isolating the one joint angle that moved, reframing proof of progress from a number a parent waits on to a movement a parent can see. This is the survivor that merges the three near-identical youth-progression before-and-afters (insider-3 and producer-5 retired into it); it carries producer-5's Strava-segment idea of treating one problem like a fixed segment where the athlete's own month-over-month line is the proof, and names the honest boundary, that it measures how the climbing changed rather than promising the next send on a schedule.
- Hook: "your climber got better three weeks before the grade did."
- Why now: The youth scene is openly arguing about grade-pressure and a documented burnout problem, and Arco week has parents watching youth competition and measuring their own kids against it.
- Critics: the single sharpest line in the youth-progression cluster, cheap and repeatable, speaking straight to a filming parent's real anxiety.

### YouTube

**four levels of the same boulder** — franchise series, boldness 4. Epicurious's four-levels engine imported to bouldering, the same problem climbed by a recreational gym climber, a youth comp climber, a national team athlete and a World Cup finalist, all on the same fixed camera and stacked as overlays so the skill gap becomes visible instead of described, with a coach naming one mechanism per level (the finalist reads and commits before touching the hold, the rec climber reads mid-move). It is refillable as four levels of a slab, a coordination dyno, a crimp line, which is rare on a slate full of one-offs, and the honest hard part is sourcing four registered attempts on one problem from a matched angle, so treat it as a deliberate flagship with Reel cutdowns, not a quick Canva ad.
- Hook: "four levels, one problem" (over a frozen four-way stack, opening cold on all four attempts starting together).
- Why now: Climbing is professionalizing toward LA 2028 with bouldering now a standalone medal, yet stays under-coached with no standard skills pathway, so a franchise that stacks the skill ladder on one problem lands as CEC builds coaching depth and a shareable movement taxonomy.
- Critics: the most reusable YouTube concept on the board and the clearest single asset for the world-stage-to-daily-gym line.

**the film room climbing never had** — video essay with Reel cutdown, boldness 3. The thesis is that talent was never the bottleneck, coaching hours were, and the sport looks self-taught only because it never built the infrastructure other sports take for granted, starting with a film room where a coach dissects one move frame by frame the way basketball has for decades. It uses Baudrand, who nearly quit, moved to a Montreal gym that matched competition and reached his first World Cup boulder final, to make the mechanism the argument, then builds the film room on screen by overlaying a Canadian athlete on a finalist's line to show the single move where tension leaks. Given the production lift, the deliverable version is a 30-second split-screen cutdown with the one-line thesis on frame, and the Baudrand and five-coach details should be confirmed current before building.
- Hook: "almost every climber you admire grew up without a coach, and the sport quietly decided that meant coaching did not matter."
- Why now: Arco is live with Canada's five-coach staff, Baudrand's Madrid round is fresh, and the first formal performance certification only arrives Fall 2026, so the under-coached argument is live.
- Critics: a genuinely good thesis that reframes the sport and rides the proven beta-breakdown format while making a claim the format usually dodges.

### Blog

**a score is not a coach** — thought-leadership essay, boldness 4. Written to be quoted and argued with by the AI-app founders it disagrees with, it reaches the position the community's own buyer's guides already reach, that you should distrust the marketing because most of these tools ignore on-the-wall technique and none read your psychology, your history or why you hesitated before the dyno, so a grade tells you where you rank without telling you what to change. It names the limitation plainly, that sportsense does not grade your climb, and argues that this is exactly the point, and it sits behind the "87 out of 100" ad as the long-form version of the same argument. Fold in the body-dependent-beta objection-handling from insider-6, that overlaying yourself on a finalist is not about copying a taller climber's sequence but about seeing where your hips, timing and foot order differ, with the concrete case of a shorter climber learning the pro loads the outside foot a beat earlier even when the reach cannot transfer.
- Hook: "the newest climbing apps will watch a video of your project and hand you a number, and that number will feel like progress right up until the next session, when you are standing under the same boulder with no idea what to change."
- Why now: AI bouldering apps matured through 2026 and the loudest current climber conversation is reflexive skepticism toward apps that score movement from video.
- Critics: a real POV that states its own limitation and makes that the argument, which is how thought leadership earns links.

**access isn't an elite problem, it's a coaching problem** — structural essay, boldness 4. It inverts World Climbing's access-over-elite rebrand, arguing that access to the sport is close to solved while access to coaching is not, and that the bottleneck sits at the coaching layer in a sport with no belt system, no standardized skills pathway and a first certification only arriving Fall 2026. It connects that to the emerging movement taxonomy (hold types, movement styles, terrain categories) a federation could standardize into a shared language, and positions sportsense as the infrastructure that reaches the climber already in a gym, standing under a problem they cannot read with no coach in the room. Confirm the December 2025 rebrand is current before building, and let this carry the taxonomy story so the field-guide idea does not double it.
- Hook: "when climbing's governing body renamed itself around access, the sport heard 'more gyms,' but the climbers who most need help are already in a gym, standing under a problem they cannot read, with no coach in the room."
- Why now: The rebrand is recent, Arco is live, LA 2028 made bouldering a standalone medal, and CEC is actively building coach professional development, so the coaching-infrastructure gap is the live structural story.
- Critics: the strongest of the infrastructure essays and a timely structural claim worth publishing.

### Email

**I love training with you but I cannot come more often** — coach unlock email, boldness 3. This is the single canonical version merged from five near-identical pitches (analyst-6, insider-5, thought-leader-5, producer-2 and the patch-notes framing of maverick-4), built on the exact sentence coaches hear, used as subject and opening line. The body names the ceiling every coach feels, that a book fully out at twenty-five in-person sessions a week runs out of hours in the day, then walks one mechanism cleanly, that the athlete who cannot come more often is not a lost client but an untapped lower-priced tier served remotely by reviewing a submitted boulder clip with overlay and voice notes, billed monthly without adding an hour on the mat. It admits the honest limit, that remote review extends the reach of hands-on sessions rather than replacing them, and lands sportsense as the two-way infrastructure the coach owns.
- Hook: subject "I love training with you but I cannot come more often," preview "the sentence that sounds like a goodbye is the revenue you keep walking away from."
- Why now: The 2026 coaching-business math spells the hourly ceiling in blunt numbers (roughly 80 dollars by 25 sessions a week caps near 8k a month) and prescribes recurring revenue billed on a schedule, and climbing athletes self-fund heavily, so a lower-priced remote tier is credible now.
- Critics: the best-built version of the line, and the one the room agreed should survive while the duplicates are cut.

## Wildcards worth the risk

**you have already stuck every move, you just never linked them** (YouTube, boldness 4). Borrows the speedrunning idea of a sum of best, splitting a boulderer's project move by move, showing that across a month of attempts they have already stuck every individual move, then having a coach assemble those best-ever clips into one ghost run and overlay it against a finalist's line so the remaining gap reads as a timing and tension problem rather than a strength one. The risk is real, the speedrun-crossover audience is thin and stitching a month of best moves into one ghost run is a genuine editing project, not a 30-second overlay. It is worth taking because "you have already stuck every move, you just never linked them" is emotionally true to every projector, it reframes projecting as connection rather than power, and it uses the tagged-attempt library in a way none of the overlay ads do, making it the road-to-2028 story in one clip.

**autopsy of a flash, cause of death, square hips at the catch** (Instagram, boldness 5). Treats one failed flash as a forensic scene, the frame frozen at the instant the send died, a white chalk outline traced around the body since chalk is the one thing every boulderer already carries, numbered evidence markers on the hand that barn-doored and the foot that popped, and a line naming the joint angle that killed it. The risk is that the coroner framing reads as mocking the climber's failure, which cuts against the voice rule that the reader must feel respected, so the fix already folded in is to keep the forensic freeze and the one-mechanism callout while softening the death cosplay. It is worth taking because the chalk-outline pun is genuinely climbing-native and scroll-stopping, and naming the exact failure frame is the honest counter to 0-to-100 scoring, speaking straight to the recreational climber who films every burn but admits the phone only shows what they did.

**some assembly required, a coordination dyno drawn as flat-pack instructions** (Instagram, boldness 5). Redraws one modern coordination dyno as a wordless flat-pack assembly manual, numbered panels with the little line figure, an exploded diagram of the sequence (bump, swing, catch, brace) and one red warning panel showing the wrong beta, square hips, the piece that never fits, making the point that a coordination boulder is read and assembled in order rather than muscled through. The risk is production, a custom illustration job a solo founder cannot pull cleanly from Canva templates, and that it reads as clever-in-a-meeting rather than something a climber saves. It is worth taking in the simpler deliverable version, a numbered-panel read laid over the real overlay, because the wobbly-bookshelf-equals-square-hips joke is also the literal mechanism, which is rare, and it lands the has-setting-gone-too-far conversation that reopened after Bern's spinning 360 dyno.

**a field guide to the movements of the modern boulder** (Blog, boldness 5). Writes each climbing movement as a naturalist field-guide species entry with its range on the wall, its habitat, its identifying marks and the movements it is confused with, every entry answer-first so AI search can extract it, doubling as the public face of a CEC-shaped skill library where clips are tagged by hold type and movement style. The risk is scope, a long multi-entry illustrated build that overlaps the access essay's taxonomy payoff, so it should ship as a tightened field guide rather than a manifesto and only if the access essay is not already carrying that story. It is worth taking because it is durable evergreen reference that meets a sport writing its own movement vocabulary for the first time, exactly the shared language a federation could endorse.

## Killed, and why

The clearest pattern in what died was redundancy, not weakness of concept. The room independently generated the same strong ideas over and over, most visibly five near-verbatim "I love training with you but I cannot come more often" emails, three interchangeable youth before-and-after progression reels, two identical reveal-one-difference-per-swipe carousels, and three versions of the same green-skeleton overlay ad, so the kills were mostly the duplicates collapsing into a single strongest build. The second pattern was channel drift, a run of genuinely good long-form blogs, essays and coach emails pitched into a session whose deliverable is Instagram-first Canva ads; those were not bad, they were simply routed to the blog and email workstreams rather than counted as ad slots, and the answer-first AEO landing page belongs behind the ads as conversion infrastructure, not as feed content. Future sessions should dedupe by message before polishing execution, and should tag each idea to its real channel early so a strong essay is not judged as a scroll-stopper it was never meant to be. The last quiet trap was currency, several ideas leaned on 2026 storylines (Baudrand's final, Anraku's three golds, the beta-spray signal, the rebrand) that must be verified live before an ad anchors to them, because a wrong "why now" ages badly in paid creative.

## If you only make three things this month

1. **guess the grade** (Instagram) — the highest-engagement, most feed-native asset, a repeatable weekly template engineered for the replay and DM-argument the 2026 algorithm rewards, with a human coach's read as the payoff that answers the AI-scoring skepticism head on.
2. **same boulder, two betas, one holds the swing** (Instagram) — the flagship overlay ad Kraig responded to live, ridden on Arco attention this week, drawing the world-stage-to-daily-gym line that is the whole strategic goal, and buildable by one person in about thirty seconds.
3. **I love training with you but I cannot come more often** (Email) — the one coach-facing piece that sells the commercial outcome, recurring revenue from athletes the coach is already losing to schedule and money, using the exact sentence coaches hear and the two-way infrastructure they control.
