# Content Think Tank: sportsense - 2026-07-21

The room met three days before Crankworx Whistler (July 24 to August 2), which is the guaranteed MTB attention peak of the summer and the anchor for anything time-pegged this fortnight. The strongest dated findings all point the same direction. Remote MTB skills coaching became a visible product category this month when Kasper Woolley launched 1:1 online coaching on Pinkbike, with the live skeptic objection ("best taught in person and in the field") sitting right there in the comments, while Ross Dunlop of GSMBC is publicly coaching Remy Metailler on Pinkbike with a drops video published in the past week. Pal Arinsal (July 10 to 12) closed the first half of the World Cup season on a 0.485 second margin for Vali Höll. On the platform side, Instagram's July update extended Reels to twenty minutes, added skip rate as a signal, and keeps DM sends weighted three to five times over likes, carousels are pulling roughly double the saves per impression of Reels, and Trial Reels (80 percent non-follower reach lift, now schedulable) remain underused. On the AEO front, 93 percent of Google AI Mode sessions end without a click, which confirms that being the cited answer, not the clicked link, is the win condition for text content.

## The slate

### Blog

**who should not use sportsense, answered honestly** (maverick-5), answer-first page, boldness 4, avg 8.3. An against-interest page under Rich's name that disqualifies readers in the first sentence (a coach with four athletes who sees them weekly does not need sportsense yet), names what they should use instead including RideReady, Lee McCormack, and the Coach's Eye alternative landscape, and only then defines the coach the product is actually for. Hook: "if you coach fewer than five athletes and see all of them every week, you do not need sportsense yet, and this page will tell you what to use instead before it tells you who sportsense is actually for." Why now: "best Coach's Eye alternative" is a live high-intent query with no honest occupant, and honest comparison structure is a verified citation format. Critics called it the cheapest, most durable asset on the slate, one no competitor will copy, with the single condition that the disqualifications never get softened later.

**how much does mountain bike coaching cost in 2026, with the real numbers** (analyst-1, merged with its near-verbatim twin insider-4), answer-first pricing page, boldness 1, avg 6.3. Opens with the answer in the first sentence per insider-4's cleaner build: a private lesson costs about $160 an hour, an algorithmic app costs $15 to $50 a month, and almost nothing exists in between, then names providers honestly (Hills MTB Skills, The Bike Farm, BetterRide, RideReady, Lee Likes Bikes, ZEP, Woolley) and explains the gap in economic terms, with every figure verified against live provider pages on publish day. Hook: the first sentence is the answer, and the gap is the problem. Why now: these 2026 prices are concrete and searchable this month, and 37 percent of product discovery now starts in AI interfaces. Critics agreed this is the correct boring AEO bet and demanded exactly one version of it ship, which is what this merge delivers.

**the trackside eye is not enough, and honest coaches already know it** (thought-leader-1), essay, boldness 4, avg 6.7. Rebuilt per the skeptic's note around the competitive fear rather than the standard video-analysis pitch: it concedes what trackside coaching does brilliantly, drops the frames-per-second physiology claim, and lands on the fact that Woolley, RideReady, McCormack, and ZEP are already selling the remote tier monthly, so a coach who refuses it is ceding their own athletes to strangers. Hook: every time a pro launches online coaching the same comment appears within the hour, and the people writing it have never watched their own riding at quarter speed. Why now: the Woolley launch made the objection live and quotable this month. Critics rated it writable in a sitting and correctly positioned as two halves of one job rather than an attack on the in-person coaches sportsense sells to.

## Wildcards worth the risk

**anatomy of a pile-up: the Mountain of Hell mass start, frame by frame** (maverick-2, youtube Short, boldness 5, avg 6.3, one kill vote). The aviation-disaster forensic treatment of the most-watched MTB footage of the summer, with the payload that riders under pressure fall to their trained habits. The risk is real and the operator named it precisely: the footage is not the brand's, the riders are identifiable amateurs, and one gleeful frame collapses it into crash porn. It survives here in a constrained form: rights cleared or organizer footage licensed, no individual rider blamed by name, and the trained-versus-improvised claim softened to what the frames actually show. If the tone discipline holds, it is the single biggest reach opportunity on the slate, because crash footage plus cold clinical structure is the most reliably watched combination on Shorts and the freeze-annotate-advance style is literally the sportsense review method demonstrated in public.

**the coach's odds board for Crankworx week** (maverick-6, Stories plus settlement Reels, boldness 5, avg 3.7, two kill votes, defended). The critics were right that ten daily markets on unsettleable propositions is a production fantasy, so this runs as the skeptic's rework: three marquee markets across the ten days, each chosen only because the footage is guaranteed to resolve it, with each settlement clip doubling as a standalone Reel. The risk is that a small account's Stories polls come back thin and the whole mechanic looks empty. The upside is that nobody in MTB runs interactive markets on technique, and every settled market quietly proves the core brand claim that technique is observable and verifiable on video rather than a matter of opinion, which is an argument no amount of asserting can make as well as one settled bet.

**occupational hazards: the parts of coaching nobody films** (producer-6, deadpan skit series, boldness 5, avg 6.3). Hyper-specific coaching business pain played straight: the cornering clip filmed from 200 metres away in the wrong orientation, the WhatsApp thread of 40 clips named IMG_4482, the platform with no way for an athlete to answer back. The risk is tonal: deadpan comedy from a founder with no proven comic delivery, in the most saturated comedy format on the platform, for an analytical brand. The mitigation is the pilot the operator and advocate both specified: shoot the single cheapest episode (the terrible clip watched in silence, opening cold on the clip itself with no POV framing), pair it with the savable filming-card asset, and only greenlight the series if a real coach laughs in testing. If it lands, "this is us" forwarded coach to coach is the exact distribution behavior Instagram now pays the most for.

## Killed, and why

Almost everything that died this week died from one of four causes. Duplication was the biggest: the room produced two identical pricing pages, two Crankworx daily slates, two waitlist carousels, and four separate assets running the same $160-versus-$184 arithmetic, so the lesson for future sessions is to check the slate for convergence before scoring, treat the duplicate as validation, and ship the strongest single version. Second, re-skins of banned territory kept sneaking back in a costume, most clearly the cue-verification carousel that three of five slides revealed to be last week's cue content, and the padel AI take that drifted back toward the AI-versus-coach frame; when a topic is ruled out, the disguised version reads as disguised. Third, several ideas were built on footage the brand does not own or on claims video cannot actually verify (broadcast frame grabs presented as measurement, an invented assertion about what Jordan Williams' team could see), which fails the truth-first rule before it fails anything else. Fourth, formats were sized for an agency rather than a solo founder, with ten-day daily production schedules and twenty-minute uncut containers that the skip-rate signal would bury. The recurring fix across all four: own the footage, tell the story once in its strongest frame, and size everything to what one person can sustain.
